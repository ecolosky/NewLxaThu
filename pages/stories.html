Theta Upsilon Stories

The Stories

I guess it's mostly about the stories.

Stories of things we did. Stories of things we wish we had done. Stories of things we wish we hadn't done. But always stories.

One of my earliest memories of Lambda Chi Alpha is from rush night, the first month of my freshman year. Along with other freshmen I was brought on a tour of the house, a tour that ended in Room 3 on the top floor. There, Pete Kelly was holding court, regaling everyone with stories of past exploits. But, he hastened to point out to us all, the idea wasn't to keep telling stories of past deeds and misdeeds. Rather, it was to go out and do new things that could become the subject of future stories.

I was intrigued.

At our Reunions, some of the best moments are the telling of, and the listening to, stories from our past. Stories that should never be lost or forgotten.

And that's what this section of the Memorabilia Vault is about--The Stories. They're introduced below by title or catch phrase. Click on one that strikes your fancy, and you'll get to read the full story.

The stories are all true; we lived them. Of course, some of them may have grown in grandeur as they grew more distant in time.

If you'd like to add a story to the collection, send one in and I'll add it. If you only have the name of a story or a fragment of a story, send that in--the colelctive mind can flesh it out.

-Roger Grice

Ronny Kowalewski Flunks German


recalled by Roger Grice

Ronny Kowalewski was taking German. (I forget which level.) He got an A on the first test, a B on the second, a C on the third, and a D on the final. His grade for the course? F.

Ronny was surprised, so he went to the professor to ask what had happened. The professor explained that F was the next letter in the series, and the grade remained F.

We All Pass 13


recalled by Roger Grice

Back in the day, Poly numbered courses by number only--no letters to identify the department. But they assigned each department a range of numbers, so once you learned the code, you could figure out what they were. EE courses were in the 400s, math courses in the 600s. Among the most dreaded ones were the low numbers--below 100--the Aero courses. The worst of all was Statics and Dynamics--course number 13. (Some sick mind must have decided to assign that number to the course.)

We were all struggling (well, at least most of us); I pulled a 6 on the midterm. Just before finals week, Professor Salkind (remember him?) showed his class eight problems that "it would be good to know how to do." Fortunately Suki and Vito were in the class. They brought the problems to the house, and we spent the weekend figuring out

how to do the problems and then memorizing the solutions.

When we showed up for the final, there were the eight problems. Almost exactly! (One was changed to work around the other angle in the right-angle triangle than we had prepped.) We managed to spit back the answers we had memorized and passed!

Lenny, Peter, Paul, and Mary


recalled by Roger Grice

We didn't have as much audio equipment then as college students do now. We did have a nice stereo that we won in a Marlboro contest. (There's another story there.) But at one point in '62 or '63, we only had two records in the house. One was Peter, Paul, and Mary's first record, and the other was a Lenny Bruce record. One or the other of them was playing in the chapter room at almost any hour of the day. So, on the way down from the bedrooms to the kitchen, we would stop in and listen to a few songs or a few skits.

Lenny Bruce was infused in our brain. The thing to do was to try to have conversations using only lines from Lenny's skits. Not easy. The most useful line was one from a skit in which a guy is locked in an airplane bathroom. When asked what was the matter, the answer was a muffled "I can't tell you." Our standard response to questions was to put our hands over our mouths and mutter in a low voice, "I can't tell you." Funny in conversations around the house. Probably not the best of responses when asked questions in class.

Celestial Manor


recalled by Roger Grice

When it came time for people to move into the fraternity house in the fall of '63, we realized that the entire top floor was occupied by seniors. (This is the same group that was the Pledge Class from Hell their first year, the Dreaded Sophomores their second year, and something forgotten but probably unspeakable their third year.) It was thus deemed that only those worthy to live there should live there. (Yes, it is reverse logic, but it sounded good.) It was decided that the floor should be given an appropriate title, one that hinted at the grandeur of its occupants.

And if we were obnoxious as freshmen, sophomores, and juniors, we were truly unbearable as seniors. (But we were worth it.)

Celeste L. Manor


recalled by Roger Grice

It didn't take all that long for a Miss Celeste L. Manor to make her appearance. She first appeared in a late-night bull session and made her second appearance on an application to join the Columbia Record Club. Records started appearing at the house for her, and we opened them for her and used them at parties. Miss Manor expanded her interest to magazine subscriptions and other free offers. The residents of Celestial Manor usually knew what to expect in the mail, but then brothers who were not residents of The Manor started filling out applications, and the flow of goodies increased.

Miss Manor was not good about paying her bills, so the freebies started to taper off. A bill collector did knock on the door one time, but we explained that it was a fraternity house and women did not live there.

Dimitri’s Liquor Closet with Lock on Backwards


Recalled by Roger Grice

When Dimitri Moursellas was High Epsilon, we really enjoyed busting his chops. (Of course, we enjoyed busting them when he wasn't High Epsilon, but that's another story.)

Dimitri had his High Epsilon closet in the basement, where he kept party supplies, including liquid refreshments. From time to time, some of the refreshments would "disappear," and Dimitri would become angry--much to the amusement of of those who had participated in the disappearance. Dimitri decided to take control, so he went out and bought a lock for the door. Not a padlock, but a fancy one like you might find on the inside of a front door.

Unfortunately, he put the lock on the outside of the door, so with a little ingenuity an a piece of plastic or cardboard, the door could be opened, so some supplies could be taken for a walk, and the door closed again as if nothing had happened. This further irritated Dimitri.

Eventually it was decided that it really wasn't fair to confiscate supplies that were needed for parties, but the scam was too good a one to stop. A compromise was reached. Before raiding the closet, we would go to the store and buy stuff--usually beer--that was comparable, but a different brand. We would then go into the High Epsilon closet and replace the beer--usually on a one-for-one basis--and lock the door.

Dimitri didn't figure out what was happening (unless he's reading this), and technically, supplies were not missing, so there was nothing to complain about.

The Wonderful Electrodes Machine (probably a collection of stories)


Recalled by Roger Grice

Pete Kelly was the owner of the machine, of course.

It was an old hand-cranked generator with two leads coming out, each ending in a hand grip. There was also an iron core that could be inserted into the solenoid or removed. When the hand crank was turned, the machine generated electricity, and the person (most often, but not always, a pledge) holding the two hand grips received a continuous electrical shock.

When the iron core was removed, the intensity of the shock increased dramatically. (At the time, we understood the physics behind why this happened, but I doubt that many of us do today.)

There are some pictures on this site of the machine in use. Typically, it was used at the chapter house, with one person turning the crank, one person holding the hand grips, and other people watching on. There's also a picture of the machine being used at a dance at Poly. A tub was filled with water, and dimes were placed at the bottom of the tub. One of the grips was placed in the water, and people got to hold the other hand grip and try to pick up the dimes at the bottom of the tub.

Kelly and I talked about the Wonderful Electrodes machine recently. Seems that it disappeared or was taken during a move from one apartment to another. But the memory of it remains.

Water Balloon Fight with Pi Kap and the Nun Caught in Crossfire


Recalled by Don Hayes and Roger Grice

Back in the day, Pi Kappa Phi's house was at 33 Sidney Place, right across the street from us. (They're no longer there; the Pi Kap chapter at Poly folded in the late '90s.) The proximity of the only two fraternities on campus to own their own houses (three others rented lofts) did make for instances of a regular Fraternity Row right in the midst of Brooklyn Heights.

The coming of spring to Brooklyn also marked the coming of water balloons. There were water balloon fights in the house, in the back yard, and occasionally with the Pi Kaps across the street. Now, water balloon fights in a classy residential neighborhood were not looked upon kindly. And to make matters worse, the Pi Kap house was right next door to the convent associated with St. Charles Boremeo Church.

You can see the punch line of this one coming, right? One of the water balloons being tossed across the street went wide of its mark. It didn't it a fraternity guy; it hit a nun. The pastor did come over and talk to both houses on that one.

We Win the Marlboro Hi-Fi


Recalled by Roger Grice and Al Ward and Silvia Giardina

Seems strange and long ago in these days where smoking is prohibited in most places, but in the 60s cigarette companies were welcome on college campus to distribute free sample cigarettes and hold promotional contests.

Roger recalls:

Marlboro was a frequent visitor to campus, and each year they had a competition: the group that brought in the most points in empty cigarette packs won a prize. (Marlboro boxes were worth 5 points; Philip Morris Commanders were worth 10 point. I even switched brands to double my point contribution.) We tried to win each year, but we didn't. AEPi managed to win each year, with a big elaborate display of bringing in empty packs--strung together in chains, piled together, etc. One year, we found out their secret: after the competition, the packs entered into the competition were throw away in bins outside Poly. AEPi went there right after the dumping and retrieved their own entries, plus the entries of everybody else. Of course, once we learned the secret, we got there first.

Thus, in 1963, we won a very nice console stereo hi-fi for the chapter room. Unfortunately we still didn't have that much money for records.

Having a much better memory, Al Ward adds:

AEPi has won the Marlboro contest before. We are told by a Lambda Chi from UMass that they have won a similar contest and have retained all the cigarette packs that won that contest. One weekend we (Carbone, Ward, and ??) drove to Amherst, Mass. and picked up those cigarette packs loose and drove back to NY. After unloading those packs on the floor of 36 Sidney, we wondered if we had enough to win our contest. So we went over to the AEPi house which was on Jay St. adjacent to a parking lot. While Ed Waldbusser served as look-out. we climbed an outside staircase to the back of the AEPi chapter house. We opened the door (it wasn't locked). To our astonishment, AEPi's cache of cigarette packs was staring us in the face. Looking down the hallway, we noticed that there were people in the chapter house. We proceeded with the heist right under AEPi's noses. We took ALL the AEPi cigarette packs. A few days later we won the contest, a stereo! Vic Carbone, Ed Waldbusser and myself were involved in the heist. There may have been a couple of other brothers involved but I don't remember who they were.

Silvia Giardina adds that her sorority also won a hi-fi in a Marlboro contest. We were persistent (or creative) smokers in those days.

After finals smoker water fight with hoses and garbage cans


Title recalled by Don Hayes, story recalled by Roger Grice, although maybe it's a different story

One of the most enduring traditions during the Golden Era was the after finals smoker, a way to let off steam at the end of the semester. It was not a particularly organized affair, but there was an official time at which a keg arrived and was tapped. People who ended finals early, or those lucky few who had no finals, often started celebrating before the official event.

The after finals smoker for the fall semester took place in January--the semester started later and ended later than it did today. It was always cold in Brooklyn then, so there was some restraint. The spring after finals smoker in June, on the other hand, was a different story. Weather was warm, and school really was over for the year.

Water activities usually started with small water balloons or mugs of water. Sometimes it escalated. Sometimes it escalated big time--hoses and garbage cans full of water were tossed around--inside the house and out. If you look through the pictures on this site, you're sure to find some of wet people standing in water that covered the floor. And the house is still standing.

Prof. Zukofsky’s Poetry Reading


Recalled by Don Hayes, Ed Horvath, and Roger Grice

Prof. Zukofski taught English at Poly. He was thin. How thin was he? He was the only person who could cross his legs, have he upper part of his legs parallel to the ground and both feet were flat on the ground. He had heavy, craggy eyebrows, that probably weighed more than the rest of him.

Ed Horvath and Cal Gomez were in one of his classes, and Prof. Zukofski announced that he was giving a poetry reading. The guys attended hoping to better their grades. It was a typical (for the day) Greenwich Village poetry reading, probably good for serious poetry buffs, a stretch for students just looking to butter up a prof. From here on the story gets sketchy, but they both passed.

Cal Gomez may be a good source of information. He took the class with Ed. Vic Carbone may somehow be involved in this one too.

The Retsina Cardinal


Recalled (vaguely) by Roger Grice and Don Hayes

One of the popular drinking games in the early 60s was "Cardinal." The game involved having a glass with some sort of drink (and the drink determined which type of Cardinal you became--Beer Cardinal was, of course, the lowest, Scotch cardinal was at or near the top). The game involved going through a carefully prescribed series of words and actions, during the course of which the drink was consumed. If any of the words or actions was done out of sequence or done incorrectly, the person had to chug the drink and start again with a fresh one. Obviously, the more a person started to lose, the less the chances of completing successfully.

One summer night, someone (probably George Kastanos) brought a bottle of retsina to the house. Retsina is a popular (or at least widely known) Greek wine flavored with pine resin. It tastes like gasoline. Not even good gasoline. It was determined that this was the night that Roger and Don would make Cardinal.

Roger went first. And even though he was not (and still isn't) the most organized of people, he managed to get through the sequence of steps and became a Cardinal.

Then it was Don's turn. Things didn't go so well for Don. He started to miss and was having trouble with the horrible taste of the retsina. Then he started having trouble with the effects of the retsina. He needed to call a halt to things.

A while later when we all got back from Coney Island and were sitting around room 7, Don left the room for a bit. While he was out, those left in the room, Roger, Pete Kelly, probably Charlie Opalek, maybe others) all poured glasses of retsina and appeared to be enjoying it when Don came back into the room. Don took one look and one whiff, turned around, and went running out of the room.

We've never really decided whether people actually ever drank retsina for enjoyment, or if it was a bad joke. But we could always gross Don out by mentioning retsina or by mentioning "retsina Fizzies." (Fizzies were tablets you could buy in grocery stores at the time. They were like flavored Alka-Seltzer--you put one in a glass of water and you had a flavored carbonated beverage. Not a particularly good one; Fizzies didn't last that long.)

Our Good-Will Event: The Annual Blood Drive


Recalled by Roger Grice

Our annual philanthropy event was sponsoring a Red Cross Blood Drive at Poly. We made arrangements with the Red Cross to come to Poly. There was a prize for the organization that got credited for the most blood donations--a keg of beer.

One of the loopholes in the rules was that you didn't have to actually give blood--just register to do so and start going through the process. If you were rejected along the way, you still got credit.

One time George Kastanos and someone else (Joe Giardina or Joe Alario perhaps?) wanted to get out of actually donating, so they ran up and down the stairs at Poly (an eight-storey building) figuring they would then get rejected for high blood pressure. They must have been in better shape than they thought--their blood pressure was fine.

One of the enjoyable parts of donating was driving the nurses crazy. While they were trying to keep us lying still while we were donating, we were sitting up and talking to other brothers across the room. As we were leaving, they gave us instructions for the next several hours (lots of fluids, etc.). One of the restrictions was no smoking for several hours. We said that we would do that, but we would be standing there with a cigarette in one hand and a lighter in the other. The one rule that was followed by pledges was that there could be no strenuous activity for a few hours. That ruled out pushups for the afternoon.

The prize keg was usually used for an upcoming party. But not always--see next story.

The Blood-Drive Prize that Went Astray


Recalled by Roger Grice

The blood-drive prize keg was usually delivered several days after the blood drive and given to the High Epsilon for the next scheduled party. But one year (and I think Dimitri was the much besieged High Epsilon at the time), there was a knock on the fraternity house door on the night of the blood drive. Stomping Cockroaches


Recalled by Roger Grice

Sometimes when we were sitting down in the Marine Grill (now part of the expanded kitchen) and the beer ran out or we just got bored, we would do The Cockroach Stop.

We would gather at the top of the cellar stairs, and on signal we would flip on the cellar light and rush down the stairs as fast as we could. The light startled the cockroaches that were wandering around the cellar, and they would scramble for cover. The idea was to stomp and squish as many of them as possible before they found hiding places. Usually we left the squished ones there. Sometimes we brought them upstairs to annoy others.

Attending the Evening Students' Smokers


Recalled by Roger Grice

Back in the day when it was OK (even expected) to have beer parties inside Poly, the evening students would have an Evening Students' Smoker each year. (Maybe even each semester.) The smoker was restricted to evening students, but there was never anyone checking at the door.

We would regularly attend these smokers, showing up in small groups, figuring that since many of us were taking night classes from time to time we were entitled. Plus, there was free beer.

Usually our presence passed without notice, but not always. I recall one time when the president of the organization spotted us and yelled out "Day Students!" or maybe a somewhat more colorful version of the phrase and came running after us to kick us out. Being younger than him, we managed to scoot out before he got to us, carrying our cans of beer with us. Not sure which was more enjoyable: the free beer or pulling a con.

Joe Giardina's Moustache Takes a Hell Week Trip


Recalled by Roger Grice

Joe Giardina grew a moustache in the early months of 1961. While he was at the house during Hell Week for Theta-Upsilon 425 through Theta-Upsilon 431, the Brothers told the Worms to shave Joe's moustache off. We tried to get out of it, but they insisted.

Cleverly, we removed the blade from the razor and while soaping Joe's upper lip, we tried to explain to him that things would be OK, that we were really on his side. We went though the motions, but, of course, the moustache stayed. The Worms caught it from the Brothers. But this was nothing compared to the ruckus the next morning when a clean-shaven Joe Giardina approached the Words screaming that we had shaved his moustache. Our denials met with scorn, and we were in deeper trouble.

At the Candlelight Party that Saturday (the party to honor the newly initiated Brothers), Joe told us that he decided to shave the moustache off but wanted to see how much of a commotion he could make of it.

In hindsight, it was very funny.

Hey! I know you--you're the traveling sec!


Recalled by Roger Grice

ELCs used to be called "travelling secretaries." And it seemed that they always came from the Mid-West or the South and were a little shell-shocked to wind up in Brooklyn. They seemed to enjoy their visits, but we always got the sense that they enjoyed leaving even more.

One semester we were expecting the traveling secretary to visit, and he arrived sometime fairly late in the evening. We brought him into the front room to welcome him and chat. Ed Horvath had fallen asleep, sitting up, on one of the couches in the front room, and the travelling secretary wound up sitting next to him. we were having an interesting time talking with him, but as we did so, Ed starting listing to the side and wound up leaning, still asleep, against the traveling secretary. Our laughter woke Ed up (sort of), and as the world came into focus, Ed looked at the guy and said, "Hey! I know you--you're the traveling sec!" We all got a chuckle, and the traveling secretary had this look of "Oh no, this place is weird."

To regain some ground, I offered to make him some coffee. He asked for it to be strong, black, and hot. Probably the wrong thing to say to me. "Coffee" in those days meant a 24-ounce beer mug with far-too-much A&P instant coffee dumped in and then boiling water poured on top. It was like mud. One mug of it could keep you awake for the a whole finals week.

I went down to the kitchen, made some mugs of coffee, came back to the front room, and gave him a mug. He looked sort of surprised at the size of the coffee, but took a sip. As he projectile-spat the coffee clear across the room, he stammered, "It's hot!" I wasn't quite sure what he had been expecting, but managed to mumble something like, "It's coffee."

Hey! It's O'Keefe's!


Recalled by Roger Grice

Frank Maez was also a travelling secretary who hailed from what we in Brooklyn called "the Mid-West," meaning anywhere west of Jersey, but not yet California. I was High Pi or House Corporation president at the time of his visit, and my role was to bring him to Poly to meet with the dean.

Frank was excited about his first trip to New York and very interested in the city. As we walked down Aitkin Place to Poly, he asked questions about the geography of the city. ("Is it really a bunch of islands connected by bridges and tunnels?") It all seemed strange to him. When we reached Court Street and made a left to get to Poly, Frank looked ahead and exclaimed, "Hey! It's O'Keefe's!" Apparently that was the one place he had heard a lot about.

Postscript: I was talking with Frank during breakfast at the 2002 General Assembly in Denver. He asked if O'Keefe's still made those great sandwiches for lunch. I had to admit that I had no idea that they served food. But apparently O'Keefe's fame is far and wide.

What's Your Name? Woo!!!!!


Recalled by Woo Han ThU 774

I came into Poly the fall of 1997. It was a fresh start. No one else from my high school came to Poly with me. The freshmen week was over and we all were heading to the NSO (New Student Overnight) trip. I was eager to make friends and a little scared. I didn't want to meet idiots, like I had in H. S.

Everyone got off the bus and was divided into cabin rooms. Our leaders was Tim Luu (TU 763) and Surva Aurangajeb (TU 766). They had fraternity letters on. But I really didn't give it a second look. I didn't come to college to join a fraternity. I was fooled by the open house and tours, that painted Poly as a fun place to go. It was the best con job anyone had done in the history of man kind.

Roger Passes German IV


Recalled by Roger Grice

German is not a language that can be spoken by people who mumble. It involves putting together more consonants than God ever wanted to be put together. I think you also need to be able to spit or drool while saying a lot of the words. Plus, many of the words are very long. If you learned to read by "sounding it out," this may not be that big a problem because the long words are often made up of smaller words all scrunched together. But if you learned to "read by sight," as I did, it can be a disaster.

I managed to pull B's in German I and II because Dr. Homberger found my attempts at the language humorous. Frau Sklar, did not find my attempts (plus my falling asleep in class) humorous at all, and I pulled the only C in a class of A's and B's. And then I wound up in her class for German IV.

The text for the class was Bertold Brecht's Leben des Gallilai, a play about Galileo. Not a word of English to be found. This story kind of shows our age: Xerox machines were new--and rare. But Poly's library had one. It also had two other things vital to this story: (1) Vito Bassi working there, and (2) a copy of Bertold Brecht's Life of Galileo, and English version of the play. Vito saved my skin.

I pretty much gave up on trying to master German and became a master of the play in English.

In class, we went around the room, each guy, in strict Germanic order, reading one paragraph from the German text. When it was my turn, one of two things happened: (1) Frau Sklar ignored my existence and went directly to the next guy, or (2) she and I struggled painfully through the first sentence of the paragraph, and then she went on to the next guy.

Class discussions were of two types: (1) fairly basic questions and answers in German, and (2) more complex, philosophical discussions in English about the content of the play. Whenever an English question was asked, my hand shot up, and I gave a lengthy, thoughtful answer. By the time the German questions were asked, I had done more than my share of talking, so I was not called on.

There were two tests: a midterm and a final. Each test was a very long passage from the play that we were to translate from German into English. I struggled through the passage until I found some German that I could recognize. I then backspaced my brain to the beginning of that passage and went ahead. After commenting that the passage was too long for a word-for-word translation, I spit back the English translation--remembering to stop at the same point that the German did I did well on both tests.

German IV was offered in the spring semester, and that year I was determined to go to Mass every day for Lent. My intentions were good, but I usually overslept and missed morning Mass at St. Charles Boremeo (on Sidney Place) and had to go to a noon Mass at a church near Poly--causing me to miss German class on a few occasions. I explained to the teacher why I had missed class, and I guess the reason seemed good. At the end of the semester, she asked me if I found the anti-Church tone of the play had offended me. I said that it did not. (This may have also helped my grade.)

I wound up with an A in the class, much to my surprise and to the surprise of some students who spoke Yiddish at home and were very, very fluent in German.

Guys from UConn Come to Visit


Recalled by Roger Grice

We got a call at the house one evening--it was Halloween or close to it--a group of brothers from the "Yukon chapter" were in the area and wanted to stop in; they were about an hour away. We didn't think we had a Yukon chapter, but we always welcomed visitors. And we thought we'd give them a special welcome.

We tied a rope to the front door and dragged the other end to the chapter room--so the door could be pulled open without anyone being there to open it. We rounded up a Halloween sound effects record and practices the parts we would play.

When the guys arrived, we had the sound effects playing, and the lighted "Lambda Chi Alpha" sign by the front door flickering (someone flipping the switch on and off). When the got to the front door, we pulled it slowly open, someone threw a plate from the top floor to the main floor so it would make a big crash, someone screamed, and Charlie Opalek tumbled down the flight of stairs and came to rest by the front door. At this point, Vic Carbone, in a black cape and elaborate black hat was supposed to run down the stairs carrying a candle and yell "Happy Halloween," but the visitors had let out a yelp and were running down Sidney Place as fast as they could to get to their car. Vic went running after them yelling "It was only a joke. It was only a joke."

We convinced them that it was safe to go into the house. They explained that they were from UCONN (University of Connecticut), not Alaska, and we sat around for the evening enjoying the beers they had brought and some we "found" in the Epsilon closet and swamping stories.

Some months later, we got a call from one of the UCONN guys saying that he would be stopping by the house and bringing some guys who had not been there before. Could we prepare another special welcome. Of course we could.

One wrinkle was that Charlie wasn't around, so I was designated as the person who would tumble down the flight of stairs. Being a novice at stair tumbling, I didn't realize that it was something that you didn't do in shorts. And Vic kept making me practice falling over and over until I got it right. (My knees and legs were badly skinned at this point.)

When the guys arrived, we all went through our paces. As I was lying face down by the front door, I could hear the visitors whispering to each other: "He's not breathing." "They must have stolen a stiff from some lab at school." They new visitor let out a yell and started out the door, while all of us and the second-time visitor laughed our butts off.

Another fun-filled evening followed.

    14 degrees Opalek


    Recalled by Don Hayes

    Charlie Opalek, Al Ward, and I were in a physics 1 class and the following problem was given.

    A cannon is mounted on a cart on a railroad track. A target is x meters away. The cart is moving toward the target at y meters per second. The muzzle velocity of the projectile from the cannon is z meters per second. At what angle must the cannon be raised to hit the target when fired?

    "The Mural"


    Recalled by Roger Grice and Don Hayes and Ed Horvath

    The story of "the mural" spans many decades.

    In the late '50s, Poly commissioned artist Andrew Tobias to create artwork for Poly. He created two stained glass panels that flanked the front (Jay Street) entrance to Poly. But his major work was to be a mural--"The History of Science" that would fill the wall that faced people as they first walked into Poly. It was to be a grand mural--large in scope, intricate in detail, and an artistic masterpiece. Tobias mixed his own paints using secret method that gave them amazing brightness and clarity.

    The project started well--near the start of school in 1960, when many of us were just starting Poly and shortly after Poly's move and consolidation from Livingston Street to the current site at Jay Street. There was a lot of excitement about the mural--so much so that canvas barriers were put up around the mural to keep nosy passers-by from disturbing the artist. There were complaints about the cost--tuition was high enough--why pay exorbitant sums to this painter?

    Progress on the mural slowed down, and a window was installed in the canvas barrier so that people could at least get some sense of what was going on.

    Tobias died before the mural was completed, and it was sort of forgotten.

    Poly received a large financial gift in the late 90s, and used some of the money to build a new building connected to the front of Rogers Hall ("The Poly" that we remember); this required creating a new main entrance, so the mural was no longer in the showcase position planned for it.

    Fast forward some decades to the 2002 Reunion. A tour of Poly was arranged as part of the reunion, and the tour guides showed us all the progress that had been made--new labs, the large new dorm, a new library in its own separate building. Some of us asked about the mural. The question drew some blank stares and then the guides realized what we were talking about. They apologized, but told us that it was a construction area that was currently locked and unavailable for viewing. At the end of the tour, we left Poly and went across the street to the Marriott hotel to congregate in the lounge.

    A while later, Don Hayes and Ed Horvath came into the room bearing large, sh*t-eating grins. Don was waving his camera. After we had left Poly, they met a construction person and explained that they had to get into the locked area. They must have sounded convincing--they got in and took the pictures included in Don's Reunion 2002 photos.

    Fast forward a year or two. A Founders Day dinner held at the Marriott featured a pre-dinner tour of Poly. We explained that we would like to see "The Mural." We were brought into the new gym that stands where the lobby once was. On one wall, behind a basketball hoop, mats were propped up along the length of the wall. The players and coaches very politely stopped the game and helped remove the mats. There was one wall of the gym, but protected by Plexiglass was the mural--unfinished just as it was when Tobias died. There were notes and sketches attached to the mural. It really would have looked good if it ever had been finished.

    Notes from Reunion 2002 (Brooklyn) Banquet Talk


    Recalled by Roger Grice

    The theme of Reunion 2002 was "Time to Reconnect--with each other, with Brooklyn, with Poly, and with the active chapter." Here are notes from the talk I gave at the reunion banquet--held in the Brooklyn Bridge Marriott (right across the street from Poly), in the evening of the day that we visited Ground Zero.

    Time to Reconnect--with each other, with Brooklyn, with Poly, and with the active chapter

    This talk is not a bunch of stories about who drank how many beers. Of course, the interesting stories are usually about what happened after we drank the beers.

    Instead, I'd like to reflect on the reunion theme: "Time to Reconnect--with each other, with Brooklyn, with Poly, and with the active chapter."

    It is good to reconnect at various points in our lives; for many of us, it has been about 40 years. But really, the connection was never broken at all.

    But much has changed.

    At Reunion 2000, we had not been together as a group in a long time. There were many changes--noticeably heavier, noticeably grayer or balder. Few of us noticeably smarter. But there had been many changes in our lives.

    It has only been two years since that reunion, so the changes are fewer, but no less important.

    Since 2000, we have learned of the death of three brothers from our era: Charlie Gomes, Mike Dilligan, and Harry Henrickson.

    Our families have increased--as new members arrived at the younger end, and diminished--as family members departed at the older end.

    In many ways, we were all affected by nine-eleven.

    Three bright spots are to be noted:

    • Chuck Schmidt has stepped up to help lead the house corporation
    • Nick Nesta, a brother from George Washington University, has stepped in to fill the vacant position of High Pi
    • The active chapter, after a weak and turbulent period, is growing and getting stronger

    It was good to able to stand as a group on our visit to Ground Zero.

    Let me briefly touch on some of our reconnections.

    Reconnecting with Each Other

    What to say? There are so many stories.

    Funny ones:

    • Blue baths
    • Olive races
    • Pete Kelly's electrodes machine
    • Ed Horvath burning down the house next door
    • Water balloons that almost made it across the street to Pi Kap
    • Road trips--that couldn't start until after night classes ended

    Meaningful ones:

    • Helping each other pass courses
    • Being at each others weddings
    • Struggling through Poly--could we have made it without Lambda Chi and each other?

    It was good to be at the house today, together as a group--in many ways still the same. We're still more rowdy than the actives.

    Reconnecting with Brooklyn

    Much of the old neighborhood is still the same.

    There are some changes:

    • The Myrtle Avenue El that clanged and screeched during class, often drowning out lectures, is gone
    • Sid's has moved
    • This hotel replaced the parking garage
    • Fulton Street is now a pedestrian mall

    Some things are constants:

    • Sidney Place
    • The house (sort of)
    • Montague Street
    • The Promenade
    • The Bridge
    • O'Keefe's (even though it has gone a bit up-scale)
    • Great food in Brooklyn

    Reconnecting with Poly

    It's still the same old razor blade factory, but it has a new front building, a separate library building, a dorm, and even some trees.

    The mural is there--but it's locked up.

    Room 116--home to those boring seminars--is still there, but it's been redone and is less crowded.

    The bookstore and cafeteria are now by the main lobby rather than tucked away in the corner and up a flight of steps.

    You need to show ID--and leave it with the guard--to get in.

    The new dorm is fancy. Will it help the house by keeping students in the area? Or hurt it by attracting students to live there rather than at 36 Sidney Place?

    New labs, new library, . . . but parts are still the same.

    Reconnecting with the Active Chapter

    It was good to meet with the undergraduates and to have some of them here with us tonight.

    At our last reunion in Baltimore, we mentioned that there had been some hard times for the active chapter. Here are some details.

    • In 1998--4 active members in good standing; we were the only fraternity on campus.
    • The chapter was scheduled no be closed down by National.
    • Don Hayes and Chuck Schmidt went to the General Assembly in Arizona to plead our case.
    • The chapter was not closed down, but a member review committee was commissioned to review each member and determine a status for each (good standing, probation, expelled), and the chapter was put under the control of an alumni advisory board.
    • Nick Nesta, from George Washington University, a member of the member review committee, agreed to serve the chapter as High Pi.
    • The chapter worked and struggled--rushed like crazy. They came to RPI for initiations (because of low chapter strength); got back to over 20 active members (a requirement set for by National); and were taken off probation.

    Currently:

    • There are two other fraternities on campus
    • Our chapter strength is decent--15 active members
    • Thales Savas and Mike Davi have been keeping the house corporation afloat
    • Nick Nesta will be leaving the area for a job in Boston

    It is time for us alums once again to pitch in and help the chapter and each other. The meeting at the chapter house today was a productive one--house corporation, newsletters, fund raiser, alumni volunteers to advise the chapter.

    Remember the enthusiasm.

    Remember the commitment.

    We no longer live in Brooklyn, but we can help financially and electronically.

    A big round of applause for Joe and Barbara Curtiss for organizing this reunion!

    Memories of Hell Weeks Past--Hell Week Schedule


    Recalled by Roger Grice

    I got to thinking of Hell Weeks of old (recently reread The Real Animal House) and was trying to patch together the many memory fragments into one coherent (OK, bad word choice there) schedule for that week of weeks.

    Here's what I remember of Hell Weeks in the early '60s, but I don't trust my memory so much these days.

    Sunday
    The week started sometime in the evening (was it 6:00?). The worms had met at a pre-arranged spot to get organized (true?). There was a formal picture of the group "in costume" and with paddles. (If we were lucky, a kindly brother took the paddles and hid the so they would not be destroyed.) Official playtime then started and went until 9:00. Then there were work projects until mid-night. That marked the end of the day, but informal playtime continued as long as anyone wanted it to.

    Monday-Friday
    During the day, people went to classes, but worms had to be back at the house whenever they were not in class (even if they only had a one hour break). Dinner was at 6:00. Worms ate out (often on Montague Street) Monday, Tuesday, and Wednesday.

    Monday-Wednesday evenings
    Study time (pretty much uninterrupted) was until 9:00. Playtime was until 11:00 (or so), and then there was worktime until whenever.

    Thursday evening
    Thursday was The Worm's Dinner-a pretty impossible event. Worms got harassed while they were preparing and serving it. Even if it was great, brothers complained and said it was lousy. Brothers were seated in strict zeta-number order; if someone arrived late, all those with a higher zeta number had to move to make room so that zeta-number order was preserved. After playtime (or as the end of playtime), the worms were brought into the chapter room as a group and asked one by one: If there were a big exam and you had to study hard to pass, but a brother needed your help in order to pass, and you could only do right by one of you, which would you choose? (Most people guessed wrong.) After this the worm's got to announce the choice of High Angel or HP. (The High Angel then got razzed unmercifully by the brothers.) Sometime during the night, there were Visitations-always interesting. At a time that was more Friday morning than Thursday night, a group went to the Fulton Fish market with Al Ward to get shrimp and fish for the Brothers' Dinner Friday night.

    Friday morning
    Friday morning, the worms brought breakfast-in-bed to brothers at very specific, specified times. The breakfast items were often elaborate and the times quirky. And because the worms were very sleep-deprived at this point, the whole things usually turned out to be a mess, and brothers who were counting on the wake-up call sometimes missed classes. Also presented at the breakfast were items that had been requested: a count of the planks in the boardwalk at Coney Island, a distilfink, etc. Friday was also a day of silence for the worms: they could not talk unless a teacher asked them a question in class. (The vow of silence hardly ever held for long.)

    Friday evening
    While the worms were out of the house having dinner, the brothers got to enjoy The Brothers' Dinner-a more relaxed and organized meal than the night before. Seating was also by strict zeta-number order. When the worms returned, a very intense playtime ensured. It started with the worms crawling, single file, down the stairs from the main floor to the basement each carrying a lit candle. Brothers yelled "Keep that candle lit," while shooting water pistols or dumping water on them. Playtime , culminating in the worms running up and down the stairs rapidly while being yelled at. Eventually they were each dragged into one of the seven upstairs rooms and congratulated: Hell Week was over!

    Some events that occurred throughout the week, not necessarily at scheduled times

    • Hell Week cocktail
    • Olive Race
    • Shoe shining (each worm had to bring a shoeshine kit with him to Hell Week; parts of it were usually "lost" during the week.)
    • Duck walking
    • Egg drop. Egg toss. Random things with eggs.
    • Carrying a raw egg to get signed by ten brothers (usually destroyed when a smiling Ronny Kowalewski announced "Oops, I forgot to dot the 'I'.")

    Spirited Mail and the Seance


    Recalled by Charlie Opalek

    During the summer, since few brothers lived in the house, we rented out rooms to outsiders to get extra rent money. One summer, an elderly lady who lived there for the summer kept complaining that her mail was being tampered with. No one ackowledged the tampering, so the running gag was that spirits were to blame.

    One evening, Ronny Kowalewski and I walked from Poly to the house with a cubic foot of dry ice carried atop an 8-foot 2x10, with each of us safely distanced on each end to prevent getting burned. Ronny, Vic Carbone, and I decided we could use the dry ice to create a cloud of mist and hold a seance to contact the spirits who were tampering with the mail.

    The dry ice did its part, and a seance-y mood was established. Just as the spirits were about to be contacted, Tony Chacharone burst into the house and loudly exclaimed "wtf is going on here?" The mood was broken, and the seance did not happen.
